With the advancement of large Text-to-Speech (TTS) models, voice cloning/conversion technologies can replicate a person's timbre realistically based on just a few seconds of audio clips, which poses a significant challenge to voice privacy protection and increases the risk of fraud. In this work, we introduce a method to mitigate the threat of malicious voice replication, and the key to this is leveraging adversarial attacks as defense. To the best of our knowledge, we are the first work to propose combating malicious voice replication through adversarial attack measures. Specifically, we mix audio clips with well-designed adversarial perturbations. The perturbations are inaudible to human ears, but sensitive to audio processing models, which makes the clip a \textsc{SneakyVoice}. The perturbed audio clips can distort the speech synthesis process and cause the target TTS model to produce a bad copy. We design an adaptive target to improve the attack success rate and propose to use relative perturbations to reduce the impact on input. Evaluation results on state-of-the-art TTS models show that our method can decrease the Speaker Recognition Similarity (SRS) to 0.41 and distort the Signal-to-Distortion Ratio (SDR) to -14.20 dB with a strict perturbation bound of 0.07. At the end, we discuss the essential policy and consensus for AI development to make our framework more universal and practical.
